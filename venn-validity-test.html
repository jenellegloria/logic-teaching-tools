Categorical Logic · Validity Testing

Venn Diagrams
as a Validity Test

Once you know how to represent a syllogism in a three-circle diagram, you can use it to test whether the argument is valid — without relying on intuition or content. The diagram either shows the conclusion or it doesn't.

The central question
After diagramming both premises, does the diagram already show the conclusion — without adding anything new?
If yes → the argument is valid. The conclusion is contained in the premises.
If no → the argument is invalid. The premises don't guarantee the conclusion.
1
Two Worked Examples
✓ Valid
The syllogism
All M are P. A
All S are M. A
∴ All S are P. A
Finished diagram (premises only)
Step 1 (universal first): "All M are P" — shade regions 4 and 7 (everything in M that isn't also in P).

Step 2 (universal second): "All S are M" — shade regions 1 and 2 (everything in S that isn't also in M).

Step 3 (read the diagram): The conclusion "All S are P" requires regions 1 and 4 to be empty. Check: both are shaded. ✓
The diagram already shows the conclusion — regions 1 and 4 are both shaded. Every S is inside P. The argument is valid.
✗ Invalid
The syllogism
All P are M. A
All S are M. A
∴ All S are P. A
Finished diagram (premises only)
Step 1 (universal first): "All P are M" — shade regions 2 and 3 (everything in P that isn't also in M).

Step 2 (universal second): "All S are M" — shade regions 1 and 2 (everything in S that isn't also in M).

Step 3 (read the diagram): The conclusion "All S are P" requires regions 1 and 4 to be empty. Region 1 is shaded — but region 4 is not. ✗
The diagram doesn't show the conclusion. Region 4 is still open — some S could be in M without being in P. The argument is invalid.
2
How to Read the Finished Diagram
If the argument is valid…
The diagram of the premises will already contain everything the conclusion requires — without you having to add anything.

For a universal conclusion (A or E): the regions the conclusion empties will already be shaded.

For a particular conclusion (I or O): a ✕ will already appear in the region the conclusion requires.
If the argument is invalid…
Something the conclusion requires will be missing from the diagram — a region that should be shaded isn't, or a ✕ that should be present isn't there.

This means the premises leave open a possibility the conclusion tries to rule out — a counterexample could exist in that unaccounted-for region.
Important reminder: Never diagram the conclusion. You are checking whether the premises alone produce the conclusion — not forcing it in. If you have to add something to make the conclusion appear, that's your signal the argument is invalid.
