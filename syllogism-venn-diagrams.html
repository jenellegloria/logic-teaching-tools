Categorical Logic · Venn Diagrams

Representing Syllogisms
with Venn Diagrams

A categorical syllogism has three terms and three propositions. To diagram it, we need three overlapping circles — one for each term — creating seven distinct regions that capture every possible combination of class membership.

1
The Three Terms
Every syllogism has exactly three terms
Subject Term
S  Subject
The subject of the conclusion. Appears in the minor premise and the conclusion.
Predicate Term
P  Predicate
The predicate of the conclusion. Appears in the major premise and the conclusion.
Middle Term
M  Middle
Appears in both premises but NOT in the conclusion. It links S and P together.
2
The Three-Circle Diagram
Standard layout — S on left, P on right, M on bottom
1 — S only (not P, not M)
2 — S and P only (not M)
3 — P only (not S, not M)
4 — S and M only (not P)
5 — S, P, and M (center)
6 — P and M only (not S)
7 — M only (not S, not P)
3
Each Premise Uses Two Circles
Major Premise
Involves S and M
The minor premise relates the Subject (S) to the Middle term (M). When diagramming, only shade or mark regions where S and M overlap — ignore P entirely for this step.
Minor Premise
Involves P and M
The major premise relates the Predicate (P) to the Middle term (M). When diagramming, only shade or mark regions where P and M overlap — ignore S entirely for this step.
4
Two Kinds of Notation
▒
Shading = Empty
Shade a region to show it is empty — nothing exists there. Used for universal propositions (A and E), which rule out entire regions.
✕
✕ = Something exists
Place a ✕ in a region to show at least one thing exists there. Used for particular propositions (I and O), which assert existence.
5
Order of Operations
Always diagram in this order:
1
Diagram universal premises first (A and E — shading).
Shading settles what's empty before you place any ✕ marks.
2
Diagram particular premises second (I and O — ✕ marks).
If one region a ✕ might go in is already shaded, the ✕ must go in the other available region.
3
Do not diagram the conclusion.
The conclusion is what you're testing — you check whether the diagram already shows it, not whether you can force it in.
